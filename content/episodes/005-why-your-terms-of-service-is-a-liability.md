Let me guess how your terms of service was written: someone on your team Googled "terms of service template," found one that looked official, changed the company name, and called it done.

I've seen this exact scenario hundreds of times. And every single time, the terms of service that was supposed to protect the business is actually creating liability. This episode breaks down why, and what a terms of service actually needs to do in 2026.

## What we cover

- **The template problem** — Why copying a terms of service from another company is like wearing someone else's prescription glasses. It might look right, but it's calibrated for a completely different set of risks.
- **The six things a ToS must do** — Limitation of liability, dispute resolution, intellectual property protection, user conduct rules, data handling disclosures, and termination rights. If your ToS doesn't address all six clearly, it's not doing its job.
- **The enforceability question** — Clickwrap vs. browsewrap. Why courts increasingly reject terms of service that users never meaningfully agreed to, and what that means for your "By using this site, you agree" language.
- **State-by-state landmines** — California, Connecticut, and New York each have consumer protection laws that can override your terms of service entirely. The clauses that won't hold up in court.
- **The 2026 update checklist** — AI-generated content, biometric data, state privacy laws, subscription auto-renewal disclosures. The issues your 2019 template definitely doesn't cover.
