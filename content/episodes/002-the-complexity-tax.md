Legal complexity isn't an accident. Someone designed it this way.

Every time you sign a contract you don't understand, every time you skip the fine print, every time you pay a lawyer to translate something that could have been written in plain English — someone benefits from that confusion. And it's not you.

This episode follows the thread from contract drafting to legislative lobbying to ask a simple question: who profits when law is hard to understand?

## What we cover

- **The readability problem** — The average commercial contract is written at a graduate reading level. The average American reads at an 8th grade level. That gap isn't an accident.
- **Three industries that profit from confusion** — Insurance, consumer finance, and SaaS. How each uses complexity as a business model.
- **The plain language movement** — A brief history of the push for readable legal documents, from the 1970s Consumer Protection Act to the SEC's Plain English Handbook. What worked, what didn't, and why most contracts are still unreadable.
- **Complexity as leverage** — How sophisticated parties use dense language to create asymmetric information advantages in negotiations.
- **What you can actually do** — Practical steps for demanding clarity in your own contracts, and how to spot when complexity is being weaponized against you.

## The question that started this

*If lawyers are supposed to make things clearer, why does hiring one usually make things more confusing?*
